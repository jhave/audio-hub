This atlas is the last step of a long pipeline. Every track in a 746-song Suno archive was scraped, transcribed, and embedded, and over several weeks the interface was rebuilt many times — lyric-space maps, audio-space maps, a four-lens interactive atlas — each version a little truer and a little more chaotic. The mixed-audio embeddings kept collapsing into one undifferentiated cloud (cosine similarity ~0.99 between almost any two tracks), so genre diversity simply would not separate. The fix was to stop analysing the mix and analyse its parts. A deeper, more granular pipeline splits every track into four stems — vocals, drums, bass, other — with a neural separator, then embeds each stem on its own and mean-centres away the shared ‘everything-sounds-similar’ component. That run took about ten hours on a MacBook Air. The four panels above are the payoff: the same music read through four different ears. The wide strip below is the old non-stem projection, kept for contrast — the hairball the whole exercise was built to escape.
